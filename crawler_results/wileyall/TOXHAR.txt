Manuscript & Metal–Organic Frameworks Single Crystal-to-Single Crystal Site-Selective Postsynthetic Metal Exchange in a Zn–MOF Based on Semi-Rigid Tricarboxylic Acid and Access to Bimetallic MOFs Alankriti Bajpai,[a] Pujari Chandrasekhar,[a] Savitha Govardhan,[a] Rahul Banerjee,[b] and Jarugu Narasimha Moorthy*[a] Abstract: The metal ions in a neutral Zn–MOF constructed from tritopic triacid H3L with inherent concave features, rigid core, and peripheral flexibility are found to exist in two distinct SBUs, that is, 0D and 1D. This has allowed site-selective postsynthetic metal exchange (PSME) to be investigated and reactivities of the metal ions in two dif- ferent environments in coordination polymers to be con- trasted for the first time. Site-selective transmetalation of Zn ions in the discrete environment is shown to occur in a single crystal-to-single crystal (SCSC) fashion, with metal ions such as Fe3+, Ru3+, Cu2+, Co2+, etc., whereas those that are part of 1D SBU sustain structural integrity, leading to novel bimetallic MOFs, which are inaccessible by conventional approaches. To the best of our knowledge, site-selective postsynthetic exchange of an intraframework metal ion in a MOF that contains metal ions in discrete as well as polymeric SBUs is heretofore unprecedented. Linking of rationally designed carboxylic acid spacers with metal ions as nodes can lead to metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) that are porous and, in some instances, with unique chemical functionality for applications.[1] In contrast to other porous materials such as zeolites, active carbon, silica, etc., MOFs permit functionalization post their synthesis.[2] Indeed, postsynthetic modification (PSM) has emerged as an exciting prospect to introduce functionality and maneuver applications of MOFs;[2,3] in general, the functionality is incorporated in the design of the organic linkers in the form of a substituent/func- tional group or introduced by changing the metal node with different properties. Recent reports have shown that the stabil- ities of certain frameworks allow partial as well as complete replacement of organic linkers and metal ions, when MOFs are exposed to the solutions containing competing ligands and competing metal ions, respectively.[3] Indeed, an instance of exchange of both metal node as well as organic linker has also been reported.[4] Though PSM of MOFs involving ligand exchange has been extensively investigated,[3,5] reports on postsynthetic metal exchange (PSME) are relatively few.[3,6] In- creasing interest in PSME of MOFs[3] is due to the fact that it permits: 1) Access to MOFs that are otherwise inaccessible by de novo approaches based on organic linkers and metal ions, 2) introduction of coordinative unsaturation through exchange of metal ions, which is important for better gas adsorption properties and development of catalytically active MOFs, and 3) creation of magnetically active MOFs. Our interest has been focused on the development of or- ganic and metal–organic porous materials based on a de novo design of building blocks that inherently feature concave shapes;[7] self-assembly of such systems has been shown by us to lead to a remarkable guest-inclusion phenomenon. We de- signed trigonal triacid H3L (see Scheme 1) as a building block with inherent concave features for metal-assisted self-assembly to access porous MOFs. The ligand H3L is characterized by a planar mesitylene core and orthogonally oriented flat aro- matic panels that are adorned at the periphery by carboxy- methyl groups. The latter were envisaged to be advantageous on two counts: first, they impart flexibility to the otherwise rigid organic linker such that the metal-assisted self-assembly leads successfully to the coordination polymers without any structurally imposed constraints; second, it is known in litera- ture that flexible organic linkers afford metal–organic materials with 1D infinite secondary building units (SBUs), thereby pre- venting the possibility of interpenetration.[8,9] Herein, we report our studies on the solvothermal reactions of the tritopic triacid H3L with Zn(NO3)2, which leads to two different Zn–MOFs, that is, ZnL–DMF and ZnL–Zn, with anionic and neutral framework structures, respectively, depending on the solvent employed. In both MOFs, the metal ion exists as part of a 1D polymeric SBU; furthermore, one observes that the Zn ions also exist as discrete 0D SBUs in ZnL–Zn. This remarkable finding, involving existence of the metal ions in two different environments, has been exploited to investigate site-selective PSME for the first time, and contrast reactivities of the metal ions in discrete and polymeric environments in coordination polymers. Treatment of H3L with Zn(NO3)2·6H2O in N,N-dimethylform- amide (DMF) and in a mixture of solvents (i.e., DEF/EtOH/H2O, 4:2:1, v/v, DEF=N,N-diethylformamide), was found to yield two different types of MOFs, that is, ZnL–DMF and ZnL–Zn, re- spectively. Heating of the solution of H3L and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O in DMF at 908C over a period of 48 h in a tightly capped glass vial led to dagger-shaped crystals of ZnL–DMF, which were found to correspond to the monoclinic crystal system (space group: Cc, see the Supporting Information). The asymmetric unit was found to contain one L, one Zn2+ ion, one DMF mole- cule, and one dimethylammonium ion. The crystal structure analyses showed that the triacid is completely deprotonated and that it is coordinated to the Zn ion with one of its charges compensated by the dimethylammonium ion. Thus, the molec- ular formula of the repeating unit is [Zn(L)·DMF·(Me2NH2)]. The coordination modes of the carboxylate groups of L are shown in Figure 1. Each tetrahedral Zn2+ ion is found to be coordinat- ed by four L spacers, out of which two carboxylate groups are found to bridge the neighboring Zn2+ ions via m2- k1O:k1O’(syn,anti) linkage on either side. The SBU thus formed is 1D, which runs down the c- axis. The SBUs are interconnect- ed by a three-connecting organ- ic spacer L, leading to the 3D open-framework structure shown in Figure 1. The channels that are formed down the c-axis are found to be filled by dime- thylammonium ions and the sol- vent DMF molecules. The MOF, in a formal sense, is anionic in nature. It is noteworthy that the p-carboxymethylphenyl rings are oriented orthogonally with respect to the central mesitylene core; the angles between the plane of the mesitylene core and the aryl rings range between 70.0 and 85.58. In contrast, heating of the solution of H3L and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O in DEF/EtOH/H2O (4:2:1, v/v) mixture at 908C for 24 h in a tightly capped glass vial, led to the formation of thick rod-shaped crystals. Single-crystal X-ray structure deter- mination of the latter revealed that they belong to I-4 space group with one L, 1.5 Zn2+ ions, and 0.5 oxo group in the asymmetric unit; three crystallographically independent Zn2+ ions, that is, Zn1, Zn2, and Zn3, were found with occupancies of 1, 0.25, and 0.25, respectively, to account for 1.5 Zn2+ ions (see the Supporting Information). Two cations, namely Zn2 and Zn3, sit on the S4-axes. In the same manner, the oxo oxygen is found to lie on the C2-axis such that its occupancy is 0.5. Thus, the molecular formula of the repeating unit is [Zn1.5(L)(m3-O)0.5]. Insofar as the coordination geometries of Zn2+ ions are con- cerned, one of the cations, namely Zn2 with 0.25 occupancy, is found to be distorted octahedral, whereas the other two are found to be tetrahedral in geometry (see the Supporting Infor- mation). A closer inspection shows that Zn1 and Zn2 are bridged by a m3-oxo group, which connects two Zn1 and one Zn2 ions. In other words, Zn2 ion that lies on S4-axis is coordi- natively bonded to the mirror- symmetry related oxo group on the S4-axis. The oxo group in turn bridges the C2-symmetry re- lated Zn1 ion with a full occu- pancy on either side. The oxo group along with the three Zn2+ ions appears approximately ’T’- shaped. The propagation down c-axis results in a 1D SBU made uniquely of two Zn2+ ions, one of which is tetrahedral and the other is distorted octahedral. The observation of Zn ions being present in two different environments in ZnL–Zn spurred us to explore partial ex- change of the metal ion selectively in one of the two environ- ments to create bimetallic MOFs by PSM approach. The site- selective exchange of one of the Zn ions, more explicitly the ZnII ion present in the discrete environment, in ZnL–Zn was explored by suspending the crystals in the solutions of metal ions such as CuII, CoII, CdII, RuIII, PdII, FeIII, LaIII, EuIII, GdIII, Pr(III), NdIII, etc. The colorless crystals were found to slowly develop coloration in the case of FeIII, CuII, CoII, and RuIII (see Figure 3). Indeed, with increasing durations of exposure, coloration in- tensities of the crystals were found to increase, thereby imply- ing gradual increase in the mag- nitude of exchange (see the Sup- porting Information). Although the exchange was found to occur with many metal ions, we limited ourselves to four cases to investigate 1) if the observed ex- change is due to a surface phe- nomenon or genuinely a meta- thesis, 2) if the exchange occurs in a SCSC fashion, 3) the degree to which the crystallinity is con- served, and 4) the magnitude of metal exchange. To begin with, the crystals of ZnL–Zn (ca. 25 mg) were sus- pended in a dilute solution of FeCl3 in DCM (5 mL, ca. 6 103m) in four independent sets. The crystals of each set were taken out and washed after 1, 2, 3, and 4 d, and examined by PXRD, SEM-EDX, and ICP-AES analyses in some cases. As re- vealed by digital microscope pic- tures, the crystals were found to crack slowly (see the Supporting Information). We were successful in determining the crystal struc- ture for the crystals suspended in FeCl3 solution in DCM for 2 d. The single crystal X-ray determination revealed that the crystals belong to I-4 space group with similar cell parameters as those of ZnL–Zn, and contain one L, 1.25 Zn2+ ions, 0.25 Fe3+ ion, one oxo group, and two DCM molecules in the asym- metric unit (see the Supporting Information). The isostructurality of ZnL@Fe and ZnL–Zn attests to the fact that the transformation occurs in a SCSC fashion, which is also corroborated by PXRD analyses of the samples at different magnitudes of exchange (see below). We wondered if Fe3+ underwent conversion to Fe2+ by some mechanism so that the charge balancing is easily taken care of. However, the ESR and Mçssbauer spectroscopic investigations confirmed that the metal ion exists in its +3 oxidation state (see the Sup- porting Information). Intriguingly, the exchange was found to be extremely slow when the crystals of ZnL–Zn were immersed in a saturated solution of FeCl2 in DCM; even after 2.5 d, only marginal exchange (ca. 1%) was observed, as revealed by SEM- EDX analysis. The exchange of a Zn2+ ion with the Fe3+ ion cannot be readily understood. To take care of the charge on the Fe ion, there must necessarily be a counter-chloride anion, which we believe must be located on the S4-symmetry as that of Fe3+ ion, which is not observed. We believe that the chlo- ride ion exists in the void spaceswith a high dynamic disorder to preclude its identity. It should be mentioned that the crys- tals suspended for longer durations, for example, 6 d, were found to undergo gradual disintegration to preclude even the cell parameters from being determined. Evidently, the crystals sustain exchange at low conversions, but collapse at high con- versions. An analogous trend was observed when the crystals of ZnL–Zn were suspended in a 0.01m aqueous solution of RuCl3; the brown crystals of ZnL@Ru obtained upon immersion of the crystals of ZnL–Zn in RuCl3 solution for 2 h showed simi- lar cell parameters; the crystals were found to disintegrate at longer durations (see the Supporting Information). In the same vein, the metal exchange was explored with CuCl2. In this case, the insolubility of CuCl2 in DCM led us to examine the exchange in its aqueous solution (0.04m). As monitored under digital microscope, the colorless crystals of ZnL–Zn were found to turn light blue-green very quickly (see the Supporting Information). Indeed, within 10 min, considera- ble coloration was observed. As in the previous case, four sets of crystals suspended in CuCl2 solution were removed at differ- ent time intervals, washed with DEF/EtOH/H2O (4:2:1, v/v) mix- ture, the solvent combination used for synthesis of ZnL–Zn, dried, and analyzed by PXRD, ICP-AES, and SEM-EDX analyses (see the Supporting Information). The ICP-AES analyses showed that approximately 15% of Zn was exchanged within 2 h and 35% in 6 h. Although the crystals were found to devel- op lateral cracks as observed under the microscope, we were successful in determining the cell parameters, but the data col- lection and subsequent structure determination were to no avail (see the Supporting Information). The cell parameters at the end of 1.5 h were found to be similar to the unexchanged parent MOF, that is, ZnL–Zn. The PXRD analyses show that the crystalline phase changes at longer durations of exchange (see the Supporting Information). The visual changes, as revealed by digital microscopic monitoring at different periods, in con- junction with X-ray single-crystal determinations of the cell pa- rameters and PXRD analyses, firmly point to the fact that the exchange is SCSC at low conver- sions, whereas the crystallinity changes with increasing magni- tude of exchange. In a similar manner, the crystals of ZnL–Zn were found to turn pink upon suspension in aqueous CoCl2 so- lution (0.04m, see Figure 3). As in the case of CuCl2, SEM-EDX analyses show that as much as 18% of Zn ions are exchanged by Co ions over a period of 6 h. The PXRD profiles also show a change in the crystallinity with increasing durations of exposure (see the Supporting Informa- tion). We note the following from all the exchange experiments carried out with ZnL-Zn: 1) The exchange with trivalent metal ions occurs more slowly than with divalent metal ions. 2) The exchange is found to occur only with those metal ions that can adopt tetrahedral geometry, which corresponds to that of the Zn3 ion in the discrete environment. Dependence of the coordination geometry being similar for favorable exchange is thus clearly evidenced from the lack of ready exchange with metal ions such as Ni2+, which are more stable in the octahe- dral configuration. 3) The crystals were found to disintegrate at longer durations of exposure. 4) The exchange was found to be irreversible in that the partially exchanged MOFs were found not to return to ZnL–Zn when immersed in the solution of ZnCl2, which suggests that the Zn2+ ions are more labile. Although X-ray crystal structure determination of the ex- changed MOF was not possible for every case, the structures determined for ZnL@Fe after 2 d and PXRD profiles for others clearly attest to the fact that the Zn ions are initially ex- changed from the discrete environment over short durations. An incisive analysis of the structures of ZnL–Zn and ZnL@Fe show that the structure is better refined in the case of the latter. Indeed, the B-factors for the metal ions in 1D SBU are not affected by exchange. Moreover, the flexible carboxymeth- yl groups oriented towards the metal ions in the discrete loca- tions become more ordered with the exchange of Fe3+ ions. The cell parameters determined for crystals of ZnL–Zn subject- ed to exchange with Cu2+, Co2+, and Ru2+ for short durations were found to be similar to those for ZnL–Zn, which suggests that the behavior observed in these cases is analogous to that found for Fe3+ exchange. It may readily be calculated that an exchange of approximately 14% of the total Zn corresponds to complete replacement of the Zn ion located in the discrete environment. Any exchange in excess of 14% means that the Zn ions that are part of the 1D SBU are also exchanged. At longer durations of exposure, the PXRD, ICP-AES, and SEM-EDX analyses in conjunction with crystal disintegration observed under the digital microscope monitoring attest to gradual ex- change of the Zn ions in the 1D SBU as well. Clearly, exchange of the metal ions in the two different environments, that is, discrete 0D and polymeric 1D, is associated with different kinetic barriers. Given that the crystal structure is conserved over short dura- tions and at low percentages of exchange that correspond to replacement of the Zn ions in 0D SBU, it can be concluded that the 1D SBU maintains structural integrity, whereas the metal ions in the discrete environment are exchanged. Of course, increasing levels of the exchange of Zn ions in 1D environment leads to crystal disintegration. A number of studies on metal exchange suggest one or more of the following as plausible reasons for its occurrence: 1) comparable ionic radii of the metal ion being exchanged and the ion exchanging, for example, Zn2+ and Cu2+ are similar, so that Zn2+ is exchanged readily with Cu2+;[6b] 2) pref- erential coordination geometry of the metal ion, as has been shown, for example, for MFU-4l containing Zn2+ ion in two different coordination geometries, namely octahedral and tet- rahedral; the ions in the latter geometry only are selectively exchanged;[10] 3) flexible coordination modes of some metal ions to exhibit four and six coordination modes so that the structural integrity is unperturbed;[6b] 4) energy gained through differences in the strengths of the coordination bonds;[3,11] 5) concentration gradient for metal exchange.[3,12] We believe that the exchange selectively of the Zn2+ ions in the discrete environment in a facile manner with Fe3+, Co2+, Cu2+, and Ru3+ ions in ZnL–Zn MOF is a result of a combination of factors that include similar coordination geometry, that is, tetrahedral, incentive in the form of enhanced metal–ligand coordination bond strengths, comparable ionic radii, and larger free volume around the metal ion in the discrete environment vis--vis that available around the metal ions in 1D SBU. A perusal of Figure 2 suggests that the Zn3 ions in the discrete environment are easy to be approached by the displacing metal ions, whereas those in 1D SBU should be relatively difficult. As mentioned earlier, exchange of the metal ion with tetrahedral geometry selectively in a MOF in which it exists in tetrahedral as well as octahedral environment is known.[10] The ZnL–Zn MOF described herein constitutes the first example of a porous MOF in which the metal ions existing in discrete 0D as well as 1D polymeric environments undergo site-selective transmetalation of Zn in the discrete environment. In general, exciting opportunities exist for bimetallic MOFs that are easily obtained by PSM, but not synthesized by de novo approaches. The selective PSME in discrete locations ena- bles access to bimetallic MOFs that contain, for example, metal ions such as Fe, Ru, etc. as an integral part of the framework structure. The Fe–MOFs should be of interest from the point of view of magnetism in such materials; notably, coordination polymers (CPs) containing Fe and Ru are very rare.[13,14] Further- more, when Zn ions are exchanged with metal ions of higher coordination number, the resultant bimetallic MOFs should be applicable for catalysis as well as gas adsorption properties due to the presence of coordinatively unsaturated centers. What is it that causes the Zn ions to exist in two different envi- ronments in ZnL-Zn? The answer should be traceable to the structural attributes of the tritopic tricarboxylate organic linker L, which features a rigid core and a flexible periphery. We are actively pursuing manifestations of analogous polydentate semi-rigid organic linkers in metal–ligand coordination in pursuit of developing MOFs with functional properties. In summary, the metal ions in a neutral Zn-MOF constructed from tritopic triacid H3L featuring inherent concave shapes, rigid core, and peripheral flexibility are found to exist in two distinct SBUs, that is, 0D and 1D, to allow site-selective PSME to be investigated. Exchange of Zn ions that are part of the discrete 0D SBUs is found to occur selectively in a SCSC manner upon immersion of the crystals in a solution of FeCl3 in DCM, as revealed by single-crystal structure determination, ICP-AES, PXRD, and SEM-EDX analyses. Analogous observations were also made with RuCl3. The PSME with dicationic salts of the metal ions such as Cu and Co ions also occurs in a SCSC fashion at low conversions, but the crystallinity is lost with higher levels of exchange. The exchange of metal ions that make up the polymeric 1D SBU leads to gradual disintegration of the framework structure, whereas it sustains the exchange of discretely located intraframework metal ions. Thus, the ap- parent difference in the barriers for metal exchange in the two different environments has permitted access to bimetallic MOFs, whose synthesis is not possible by de novo approaches. To the best of our knowledge, site-selective postsynthetic exchange of intraframework metal ions in a MOF that contains the metal ions in two different SBUs is heretofore unprecedented. Experimental Section Preparation of ZnL–DMF: A solution of H3L (0.025m) in DMF (3.0 mL) was mixed with Zn(NO3)2·6H2O in DMF (1.0 mL) in a tightly capped glass vial. The contents were heated at 908C. Dagger- shaped crystals of ZnL–DMF were obtained after 48 h: yield 81% (0.019 g, 0.025 mmol). Preparation of ZnL–Zn: A solution of H3L (0.20 g, 0.35 mmol) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.21 g, 0.70 mmol) in 14 mL of DEF/EtOH/H2O (4:2:1, v/v) mixture was heated at 908C in a tightly capped glass vial. After 20 h, long columnar crystals of ZnL–Zn were obtained: yield 98% (0.23 g, 0.34 mmol). Metal Exchange Studies: In a typical experiment, 25 mg of ZnL– Zn MOF was suspended in the solution of metal salt. For example, the MOF crystals were suspended in DCM (5.0 mL) containing FeCl3 (5.0 mg). The crystals, after metal exchange, were isolated and thoroughly washed with DEF/EtOH/H2O (4:2:1, v/v) mixture after 1, 2, 4, 6, and 10 d. The exchange with Ru3+ was performed by sus- pending the crystals of ZnL–Zn in RuCl3 (2.0 mL of 0.01m aqueous solution). Likewise, exchange with Cu2+ and Co2+ ions was carried out by using approximately 0.04m aqueous solutions. All the crys- tals upon isolation from the solution of the exchanging metal salt were air dried for 24 h and dried at 1008C for another 24 h prior to SEM-EDX and ICP-AES analyses. The individual parameters and refinement conditions for each case are recorded in separate tables. The intensity data were collected on a Bruker APEX II CCD detector system with Molybdenum sealed tube (=0.71073 Å) and highly oriented graphite monochromator. The lattice parameters and standard deviations were obtained by a least squares fit using 25 frames with 20 sec/frame exposures with the Bruker APEX2[1] software. The data were collected by phi and omega scans with 2 = 40º and ~10 sec/frame exposures to collect a hemisphere of data. Data processing and reduction was done using Bruker SAINT[1] and empirical absorption correction made using Bruker SADABS.[1] The structures were solved and refined using the using WINGX[2] and SHELXLe-2014[3] programs. The hydrogen atoms were included in the ideal positions with fixed isotropic U values and were riding with their respective non-hydrogen atoms. The latter were refined before inputting to the SQUEEZE[4] and PLATON[5] software. The structures with Cu and Co metal exchange, i.e., ZnL@Cu and ZnL@Co, were not easy to handle, as part of the structure was found to be disordered. Indeed, the two structures could not be refined to completion.
