Graphical abstract Two new coordination polymers, [Zn2(mal)(1,10-phen)Cl] n (1) and [Cd(glut)(1,10-phen)(H2O)] n (3), have been synthesized under hydrothermal condition. Furthermore, complex 1 and complex 3 exhibit intense photoluminescent property at room temperature. A new coordination polymer, [Zn2(mal)(1,10-phen)Cl] n (1), (mal=malate, 1,10-phenanthroline), has been synthesized with malic acid and fumaric acid which are generated from maleic acid under hydrothermal reactions. At about the same condition, we get [Cd(fma)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)] n (2) (fma=fumarate, 2,2′-bpy=2, 2′-bipyridine). The diverse products illustrate that the carbon–carbon doublebond of the maleic acid has two kinds of reaction trends under different conditions. Complex 1, which displays a two-dimensional (4, 8) lattice-type network, is formed from Zn and maleic through the addition reaction with water molecule. If the Zn is changed by Cd, at the same reaction condition with 1, a two-dimensional supramolecular network complex 2 is formed through the conformation transform reaction. To our knowledge, a lot of coordination polymers have been constructed from malic acid and fumaric acid directly; however, these kinds of complexes have seldom been synthesized from maleic acid under hydrothermal reaction. As is known, the rigid carbon–carbon double bond makes maleic acid lead to some unique structural features which the saturated aliphatic acid does not possess. To illustrate this clearly, a simple one dimensional complex 3, [Cd(glut)(1,10-phen)(H2O)] n (glut=glutarate), is synthesized. Furthermore, complex 1 and complex 3 exhibit intense photoluminescent property at room temperature. Keywords Maleic acid Hydrothermal reaction Coordination polymer Mixed ligands 1 Introduction An enormous expansion of the research field of coordination polymers based on metal–organic ligand coordination is motivated by the interest in creating structures with cavities and channels of potential use in nanotechnology, shape and size selectivity catalysis, separations and molecular recognitions [1–5]. Rigid multi-carboxylate ligands, such as 1,2-benzenedicarboxylate, 1,3-benzenedicarboxylate, 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate, 1,2,4-benzenetricarboxylate, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate, have been exploited to generate novel different dimensional metal–organic hybrid compounds [6–9]. Recently, growing attention has been paid to rational design and synthesis of coordination polymers by utilization of saturated aliphatic carboxylate, since the flexibility and conformational freedom of such ligands could give rise to a variety of interesting structures [10,11]. Compared with two kinds of ligands mentioned above, unsaturated aliphatic carboxylate, such as maleic acid and fumaric acid, has seldom been researched [12]. To our knowledge, the unsaturated aliphatic acid exhibits several merits which the rigid multi-carboxylate acid and saturated aliphatic acid do not possess: firstly, they have less conformational freedom due to the CC double bond, secondly, they have two carboxylate groups, which can adopt various coordination manners [13]. Moreover, the CC double bond is an active group, which can lead to the formation of a number of reactions under certain conditions, such as the conformation transform (from cis to trans) and adding with water molecule under the catalysis of Lewis acid. This makes it act as a reaction active point, which can lead to some interesting situ-position reactions [14]. As we all known the ligands generated at hydrothermal reactions can lead to some novel structural features such as interpenetrating, chirality and others. Based on this point, we think the ligands generated at this condition can lead to some structures, which cannot be obtained from normal ligands directly. On the other hand, polynuclear d10 metal (CuI, AgI, AuI, ZnII, CdII) complexes have attracted extensive interest in recent years in that they exhibit appealing structure and possess photo luminescent properties [15]. Based on the aforementioned points, we synthesized two interesting coordination polymers: [Zn2(mal)(1,10′-phen)Cl] n (1) and [Cd(fma)(2,2′-bpy)(H2O)] n (2). Furthermore, as we all known carbon–carbon double bond can introduce a lot of interesting structures, which cannot be constructed by carbon–carbon single bond. To test this point, we introduced glutaric acid ligand and synthesised a simple two-dimensional supramolecular network [Cd(glut)(1,10′-phen)(H2O)] n (3). 2 Experimental 2.1 Materials and methods All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and were used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, N, H) were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. FT/IR spectra were recorded in the range 4000–400cm−1 on an Alpha Centaut FTIR spectrophotometer using a KBr pellet. Photoluminescence spectra were measured using a FL-2T2 instrument (SPEX, USA) with 450-W Xenon lamp monochromatized by double grating (1200gr/mu). All measurements were performed at room temperature. 2.2 Syntheses of complexes 1 to 3 2.2.1 [Zn2(mal)(1,10′-phen)Cl]n (1) Complex 1 was prepared from the mixture of ZnCl2 ·6H2O (0.061g, 0.25mmol) maleic acid (0.067g, 0.50mmol), phen (0.090g, 0.50mmol) and 8ml H2O, was stirred for 30min, and then the pH value was adjusted to 5 with 1M NaOH. After stirring for another 30min, the mixture was transferred to a 23ml Teflon-lined stainless steel bomb and kept at 160°C under autogenous pressure for 5 days. The reaction system was cooled to room temperature during 24h. A large amount of yellow block crystals of 1 were obtained. Yield: 51% (base on Zn). Elemental Anal. Anisotropic thermal parameters were used to refine all non-hydrogen atoms. Hydrogen atoms were located from difference Fourier maps. Crystal data and structure refinement, atomic coordinates, bond lengths and angles, and anisotropic displacement parameters are available in supplementary crystallographic data. 3 Results and discussion 3.1 Structure descriptions The single X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 displays a three-dimensional supramolecular structure composed of two-dimensional covalent network. The fundamental unit is shown in Fig. 1 a. There are two crystallographic independent Zn centers in the asymmetric unit. Zn(1) adopts tetrahedron coordination mode connected with three oxygen atoms:two carboxyl oxygen atoms from different malic acid with the Zn–O distance ranging from 1.956 to 2.001Å, one hydroxyl oxygen (Zn–O distance is 1.945Å), and one Cl atom with the bond distance 2.209Å. The O–Zn(1)–O distances are in the range of 95.37(10) to 113.21(11)°, and the O–Zn(1)–Cl bond angles range from 111.14(8) to 115.18(10)°. The Zn(2) coordinated with two carboxyl oxygen atoms from different malate acid with Zn(2)–O distance 2.086 and 2.104Å, respectively, one hydroxyl oxygen atom (the bond length is 1.988Å), and one phen molecule with the average Zn(2)–N bond distance is 2.130Å. The malic acid ligand exhibits an interesting connection mode with metal ions. Both carboxylate groups display bridging connection mode and link with two crystallographic independent zinc atoms, respectively, at the same time, the hydroxyl oxygen atom bridges two different zinc atoms together as seen in Fig. 1b. That is to say, every malic acid ligand connects with four zinc atoms. A pair of zinc atoms links two such malic acid ligands with hydroxyl oxygen atom being in the face to face mode to form a eight membered ring with dimensions 9.746×6.022Å. Four such eight membered rings are connected by four different zinc atoms to generate a four membered rings. On such coordination mode, an interesting two-dimensional (4,8)-lattice-type network is formed with 8 and 4 number ring distributed alternately as shown in Fig. 1c. Furthermore, two such adjacent layers are connected by π–π interactions to form a three-dimensional supramolecular network with C(2)–C(8)=3.558Å, and C(3)–C(9)=3.603Å) (see Table 3 ). During the reaction processes, what interested us is the malic acid ligand coordination to zinc atoms directly. As no malic acid was added to the starting reaction mixture, we think that the maleic acid reacts with water molecules to produce chiral malate ligand. Unlike other adding reactions, which produce both R- and S-configuration malate acid, in this reaction, only R-configuration malate acid ligand was found; we deduce that a stereo-specific reaction must have taken place in this process. To our knowledge, no documented example upon maleic ligand of such a phenomenon has been reported under the same reaction conditions (see Fig. 2 ). Complex 2 displays a two-dimensional supramolecular structure composed of one-dimensional covalent chains as reported by X, He and C, Z, Lu. Here, we synthesis it use maleic acid. We suppose that the conformation of the carbon-carbon double bond converted from cis mode to trans mode under hydrothermal reaction. To our knowledge, this kind of convention has never been reported under the same condition. The single X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 3 displays a two-dimensional supramolecular structure composed of one-dimensional chains. The fundamental unit is shown in Fig. 3 a. There is one crystallographic independent Cd center in the asymmetric unit. The Cd center is coordinated to four oxygen atoms (O(1), O(2), O(3) and O(4)) from two different glutaric ligands with the Cd–O distance from 2.312 to 2.511(2)Å, and the O–Cd–O angles are in the range of 54.79–144.65°. At the same time, the Cd center connects two N atoms from a phen molecule (Cd(1)–N(1)=2.381Å and Cd(1)–N(2)=2.377Å). Another coordination site is occupied by a water molecule with a distance of 2.323Å. The adjacent Cd centers are connected by glutaric ligand to form a one-dimensional chain along b axis as shown in Fig. 3b. Two neighboring chains are linked by a hydrogen bond (with the O(5)–H(1)…O(3)=2.728Å) to form a double chain with the phen molecules at the outside. With the π–π interactions (C(7)–C(10)=3.623Å, C(9)–C(12)=3.642Å) between the phen molecules from different double chains, a two-dimensional layer is formed parallel with bc plane as shown in Fig. 3c. 3.2 IR Spectroscopy For complex 1, the absorption peaks at 1559cm−1 and 1407cm−1 display the asymmetric and symmetric vibrations, respectively; the Δ is 152cm−1. This value is consistent with the coordination mode of this complex. For complex 3, the separations (Δ) between γ asym(CO2) and γ sym(CO2) indicate the presence of chelating (80cm−1) and bridging (166cm−1) coordination modes of carboxyl group. The peak at 3421cm−1 represents the existence of water molecules in it [16]. 3.3 Thermogravimetric analyses The thermal stability of the three complexes has been determined on single crystalline samples in a nitrogen atmosphere by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). For complex 1, TGA shows that chemical decomposition starts at 361°C and ends at 427°C with a weight loss of 72.19%, which is equivalent to the loss of ligand molecules (Calculated: 72.56%), the remaining weight loss corresponds to ZnO. The TG curves of complex 3 exhibit two steps of weight losses. The first weight loss occurs in the range of 150–210°C, corresponding to the removal of coordinated H2O molecules. The observed weight loss (4.13%) is consistent with the calculated value (4.08%). The second weight loss is 70.49% from 220 to 440°C, assigned to the loss of phen and glutaric molecules. The observed weight loss in the second step is in agreement with the calculated value (70.34%). 3.4 Photoluminescence property The emission of complex 1 in the solid state at room temperature is shown. It can be observed that complex 1 exhibits intense blue photoluminescence with an emission maximum at ≈413nm upon excitation at 373nm. For complex 3, it exhibits photoluminescence with an emission maximum at ≈454nm upon excitation at ≈351nm as seen in Fig. 4 . This observation indicates that all the four complexes may be excellent candidates for potential photoactive materials [17]. 4 Conclusions In this article, we employ d10 metal, maleic acid and chelating N containing ligand under the hydrothermal conditions to synthesise three supramolecular complexes. To our interest, under hydrothermal conditions, maleic acid turned into malic acid and fumaric acid, respectively. Complex 1 and complex 3 exhibit strong photoluminescence property at room temperature.
