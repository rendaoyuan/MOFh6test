(2) and [Zn3(dcp)2(H2O)5]? (3) with the ligand 3,5-pyrazoledicarboxylic acid were prepared by the hydrothermal method. The complexes were structurally characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectroscopy and X-ray single-crystal diffraction. Compound 1 is a mono- nuclear molecule that is linked into a 3D supramolecular framework via N–HO and O–HO hydrogen bonds. In 2, two types of carboxylic bridges were found between ZnII ions to form a 1D double-chain. The 1D chains were fur- ther construct into a 3D structure by hydrogen bonds. Trinuclear 3 consists of 1D bi-inﬁnite parallelogram chains of [Zn3(dcp)2(H2O)3] trimers. In the basic trinuclear unit a further bridging mode of the ligand is seen where two dcp3- ligands chelate three ZnII ions by utilizing ﬁve donors of the dcp3- ligand. The photoluminescent prop- erties of 1, 2 and 3 were investigated. Keywords 3,5-Pyrazoledicarboxylic acid  Zinc(II)  Crystal structure  Photoluminescence 1 Introduction Recent years have witnessed an explosion of interest in both molecule-based photoluminescent properties and supramolec- ular chemistry [1–4]. A series of transition metals have been used in cases where linker-based photoluminescence is observed. The most commonly reported structures are for ZnII and CdII, which have ﬁlled, core-like d-orbitals; and, thus no d–d transitions are possible [5–8]. Lots of organic aromatic polycarboxylate ligands such as 1,3-benzenedicarboxylate, 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate or 2,20- biphenyldicarboxylate have been extensively applied in the construction of a rich variety of metal–organic frameworks [9–15]. Only the oxygen atoms of these molecules can act as coordinating atoms and hydrogen donors/acceptors, which enable the formation of metal–organic supramolecules. 3,5-Pyrazoledicarboxylic acid (H3dcp) is a multifunctional ligand. The versatile nature of this ligand has potential coor- dination sites involving the two N-atoms of the pyrazole ring and the four carboxylic O-atoms; therefore, fully deprotonated H3dcp has six donor sites. As proton donors and acceptors, the O-atoms of carboxylic groups and the N-atoms of pyrazole in H3dcp do not only coordinate metals to form monodentate and/ or multidentate M–O and M–N bonds, but also provide inter- molecular hydrogen bonds for assembling the coordination compounds into 3D supramolecular motifs. A variety of coordination compounds based on H3dcp-containing transition, lanthanide and alkaline-earth metals has been synthesized and reported [16–22]. The relevant hydrogen-bonding parameters are summarized in Table 3. The only difference in the synthetic conditions for 1–3 is the pH of the reactions. Clearly, the pH value for the reaction plays a key role in controlling the structures of the complexes [21, 22, 25, 26]. By varying the pH, the coordination mode for the H3dcp ligand changes; i.e., three different structural complexes occur. Speciﬁcally, the protonated and/or depro- tonated carboxyl groups and the N-atom of pyrazole depend on pH. Thus, the H3dcp ligand exists in partially and fully deprotonated forms in solution; e.g., H3dcp $ H2dcp- ? H? $ Hdcp2- ? 2H? $ dcp3- ? 3H?. At pH 5 or 7, only the two carboxyl groups of ligand are deprotonated (Hdcp2-) in 1 and 2. At higher pH 9, all of the carboxyl groups and the pyrazole N-atom (N2) are deprotonated (dcp3-), which favors the formation of 3. Furthermore, the connecting pattern of the H3dcp ligand also depends on pH. For 1–3, an increase in pH results in higher connectivity of H3dcp. For 1, the H3dcp ligand coordinates to one ZnII-atom in the g2 (N, O) mode. For 2, three ZnII-atoms are in the g4 (N, 3O) and for 3, three ZnII- atoms are in the g5 (2 N, 3O) mode (Scheme 1). To characterize 1, 2 and 3 further, TGA studies were carried out in air from 30 to 800 C (Fig. 7). For 1, the loss of free and coordinated water molecules takes place from 115–265 C (mass loss: obsd. 29.6 %, calcd. 31.1 %). The removal of H3dcp ligands from the intermediate product is observed from 350–620 C (mass loss: obsd. 49.0 %, calcd. 48.4 %). For 2 and 3, the ﬁrst weight loss (110–260 C and 105–220 C, respectively) is 13.8 and 15.4 % are assigned to the release of the coordinated water molecule (calcd. 14.1 and 15.2 %). The decomposition of the H3dcp ligands takes place from 305–615 C (mass loss: obsd. 59.9 %, calcd. 60.3 % for 2 and obsd. 51.5 %, calcd. 51.7 % for 3). The ﬁnal products are probably ZnO. Although the composi- tions of these three complexes are similar, the TGA are slightly different. The difference may be the effect of coordinated mode. ZnII is well known as an ideal metal center for building coordination polymers with luminescence properties, since the metal has a d10 electronic conﬁguration. The d electrons are in the valence orbitals, which enables metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) [4]. The photoluminescent prop- erties of 1–3 were investigated at room temperature (Fig. 8). The emission peaks are at 487 nm (1), 506 nm (2) and 503 nm (3) upon excitation at 365 nm. To understand the nature of these emission bands, the photoluminescence of the free ligand H3dcp was examined and exhibited a strong emission peak at *434 nm. The reason for different emissive peak positions of three materials is presumably a result of the differences in coordination mode of the complexes. 4 Conclusions Three ZnII coordination complexes based on H3dcp ligand were prepared by controlling the reaction pH under hydrothermal conditions. Various coordination conforma- tions and deprotonation of the H3dcp ligand were found and were inﬂuenced by pH. The method represents an excellent example of the acidity-controlled polymeric architecture construction. Moreover, hydrogen-bonding interactions play an important role in connecting the 0D cluster unit or 1D chains into extended 3D supramolecular networks.
