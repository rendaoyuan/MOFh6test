Turner1* The synthesis and structural, magnetic and gas adsorption properties of a series of coordination polymer materials prepared from a new, highly flexible and internally functional tetrakis-carboxybenzyl ligand H4L derived from 1,2-diaminoethane have been examined. The compound poly- [Ni3(HL)2(OH2)4]¬∑2DMF¬∑2H2O 1, a two-dimensional coordination polymer, contains aqua- and carboxylato-bridged trinuclear NiII clusters, the magnetic behaviour of which can be well described through experimental fitting and ab initio modelling to a ferromagnetically coupled trimer with a positive axial zero-field splitting parameter D. Compound poly-[Zn2L]¬∑2DMF¬∑3H2O 2, a three- dimensional coordination polymer displaying frl topology, contains large and well-defined solvent channels, which are shown to collapse on solvent exchange or drying. Compound poly-[Zn2(L)(DMSO)4- ]¬∑3DMSO¬∑3H2O 3, a highly solvated two-dimensional coordination polymer, displayed poor stability characteristics, however a structurally related material poly-[Zn2(L)(bpe)(DMSO)2]¬∑DMSO¬∑3H2O 4 was prepared under similar synthetic conditions by including the 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene bpe co-ligand. Compound 4, containing small one-dimensional solvent channels, shows excellent structural resilience to solvent exchange and evacuation, and the evacuated material displays selective adsorption of CO2 over N2 at 273K in the pressure range 0 ‚Äì 1 atm. Each of the coordination polymers displays subtle differences in the conformation and binding mode of the ligand species, with switching between two distinct conformers (X-shaped and H-shaped), as well as a variable protonation state of the central core, with significant effects on the resulting network structures and physical properties of the materials. Introduction In recent years, interest in coordination polymer materials, and their porous sub-group Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs),1 has expanded beyond the synthesis of materials as scientific curiosities, and is evolving towards a wide variety of potential real-world applications.2 The properties of polymeric metallosupramolecular assemblies have been probed to determine their feasibility for applications such as gas storage and separation, molecular magnetism-based memory applications, degradation of chemical warfare agents, small molecule separation and detection, heterogeneous catalysis and non-linear optics.3 A common facet amongst any foreseeable technical applications of coordination polymer materials, and one reason for the intense interest directed towards the field, is the notion of chemical customisability.4 The ability to continuously tune the chemical functionality and nanoscale geometric properties of coordination polymer materials, both porous and non-porous, represents an unprecedented advance in materials science.5 These properties make MOFs particularly attractive in the field of carbon dioxide capture, an area of great environmental importance.6 Recently, substantial advances have been reported in the use of amine-containing frameworks for highly selective and efficient carbon dioxide capture from mixed gas streams, and substantial interest remains in the synthesis of amine-derived porous coordination polymers.7 To date, the majority of porous coordination polymers reported have been derived from rigid and symmetrical ligand molecules, typically consisting of an aromatic core with carboxylate or other donor groups attached.8 This design is most often chosen due to the propensity for rigid aromatic linkers, often in combination with secondary-building units, to form robust porous materials whose framework integrity is maintained on solvent exchange and evacuation.9 Flexible linkers are more commonly associated with either close packed structures (based on the ability of flexible ligands to distort to Page 1 of 17 Dalton Transactions Dalton Transactions Accepted Manuscript Published on 17 Experimental Materials and Methods All reagents, solvents and starting materials were obtained from Sigma Aldrich, Alfa-Aesar or Merck and were used as received. NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AVANCE spectrometer operating at 400 MHz for 1H and 100 MHz for 13C nuclei, with all samples dissolved in deuterated solvents as specified and signals referenced to the residual non-deuterated solvent peak. Melting points were recorded in air on an Electrothermal melting point apparatus and are uncorrected. Mass spectrometry was carried out using a Micromass Platform II ESI-MS instrument operated in positive and negative ionisation mode. Thermogravimetric analyses were carried out with a Mettler-Toledo STARe TGA/DSC instrument with samples in aluminium crucibles of 40 ¬µL capacity heated from 30 ‚Äì 400 ¬∞C at a rate of 5 ¬∞C/min under a nitrogen purge flow of 20 mL/min. Microanalysis was performed by Campbell Microanalytical Laboratory, University of Otago, New Zealand. Infrared spectra were obtained using an Agilent Cary 630 spectrometer equipped with an Attenuated Total Reflectance (ATR) sampler. Bulk phase purity of all crystalline materials was confirmed with X-ray powder diffraction patterns recorded with a Bruker X8 Focus powder diffractometer operating at Cu KŒ± wavelength (1.5418 √Ö), with samples mounted on a zero- background silicon single crystal stage. Scans were performed at room temperature in the 2Œ∏ range 5 ‚Äì 55¬∞ and compared with predicted patterns based on single crystal data (ESI‚Ä†). Gas adsorption analyses were carried out using a Micromeritics TriStar 3020 volumetric analyser, with samples prepared by evacuating under dynamic vacuum overnight at the temperatures specified, using a Micromeritics Vacprep 061 station. Ultra high purity gasses were used for all analyses. Temperature control was achieved using insulated ice water (273 K) or liquid nitrogen (77 K) baths, or a temperature controlled water recirculator (302 K). Enthalpy of adsorption calculations were performed by fitting a Virial-type equation to the adsorption data at two temperatures (Supporting Information). Magnetic susceptibility measurements were made using a Quantum Design MPMS 7 Squid magnetometer. The accurately weighed (~20 mg) sample of the nickel(II) compound, 1, was contained in a gel capsule that was held at the centre of a drinking straw, the latter fixed to the end of the sample rod. Fields of 1T and 0.1 T were used. The instrument was calibrated by use of an accurately known pellet of palladium supplied by Quantum Design. Magnetization measurements were between 2 ‚Äì 20 K in fields of between 0 ‚Äì 5 T. Ab initio Complete Active Space Self-Consistent Field (CASSCF) calculations for 1 were performed using MOLCAS 8.0.13 The X-Ray structure was used without optimization and each crystallographically independent Ni site was treated separately to extract the local electronic properties, where the Ni ions not of interest were substituted with the diamagnetic Zn(II). An active space of 8 electrons in 5 3d orbitals was employed, where all 10 triplets and 15 singlets were used for both the RASSCF orbital optimization and the RASSI spin- orbit mixing procedures. Basis sets from the ANO-RCC library were employed exclusively,14 with VTZP quality for the Ni ion, VDZP quality for the first coordination sphere (including H atoms of bound water molecules) and VDZ quality for all other atoms. The two electron integrals were Cholesky decomposed. Results and Discussion Structure of H4L The ligand H4L was prepared by the reaction of methyl 4- bromomethylbenzoate with ethane-1,2-diamine and hydrolysis of the resulting tetra-ester precursor Me4L (Scheme 1). The white solid obtained on precipitation with hydrochloric acid was purified to the neutral anhydrous form H4L by recrystallization from a 1:1 DMF:H2O mixture. The crystals of H4L obtained by this method were of sufficient quality to allow analysis by single crystal X-ray diffraction, where a structure model was obtained in the triclinic space group P-1. The asymmetric unit of H4L contains the molecule in its entirety, and is devoid of any inorganic species or solvent molecules. The molecule adopts an H-shaped conformation, where the two benzoate arms attached to each nitrogen atom adopt divergent orientations (Figure 1). The shortest carbon¬∑¬∑¬∑carbon distances of 3.436(2) and 3.492(2) √Ö between aromatic rings originating from the two unique nitrogen atoms indicates some degree of intramolecular œÄ-œÄ interaction accompanying the tweezer-like orientation of the two sets of aromatic rings. Fig. 1 Structure of H4L with heteroatom labelling scheme. Carbon-bound hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity. Intermolecular interactions within the structure of H4L primarily consist of hydrogen bonds originating from the carboxylic acid groups. Despite the basicity of the two tertiary nitrogen atoms within the molecule, all four carboxylic acid groups exist in their protonated states, and each engages in an R (6) carboxylic acid dimer-type interaction with a symmetry related molecule.25 Two such interactions exist between each pair of H4L molecules, with the result that the extended structure of H4L takes the form of an undulating one- dimensional chain with four prominent hydrogen bonds (all D- A distances in the range 2.593(2) ‚Äì 2.661(2) √Ö) between each pair of molecules, as shown in Figure 2. Presumably as a result of these interactions in the solid state, the crystals of H4L displayed a melting point in excess of 300 ¬∞C and poor solubility under ambient conditions. Only modest œÄ-œÄ interactions are observed between chains of H4L, while the polar tertiary amine sites do not experience any significant intermolecular interactions, most likely by virtue of the steric hindrance provided by the ortho-hydrogen atoms from the attached phenyl rings and the lack of any unbound strong hydrogen bond donors. Magnetic behaviour of poly-[Ni3(HL)2(OH2)4]¬∑2DMF¬∑2H2O 1 The plot of œáMT, per Ni3, versus temperature for 1 is shown in Figure 5. Between 300 and ~ 60 K, œáMT remains constant at 3.85 cm3 mol-1 K (1.28 cm3 mol-1 K; ¬µeff = 3.20 ¬µB, per Ni). The œáMT value then increases with decreasing temperature in a field, B, of 1 T, to reach a maximum of 4.61 cm3 mol-1 K at 4.5 K, typical of ferromagnetic coupling, then rapidly decreases to reach 3.90 cm3 mol-1 K at 2 K. In a field of 0.1 T, below ~10 K, the œáMT values increase to reach 5.2 cm3 mol-1 K at 2 K, this field dependent behaviour being due to Zeeman depopulation effects. Long range magnetic ordering could be ruled out since the FC and ZFC susceptibilities were identical between 2 - 60 K (Fig S1, ESI). The isothermal magnetisation plots in fields of 0 ‚Äì 5 T over the range 2 ‚Äì 20 K, shown in Figure 5, reach M = 6.20 N¬µB at 2 K and 5 T without reaching saturation. This is indicative of a S = 3 coupled ground state with a g value slightly larger than 2. Corresponding reduced magnetisation plots of M vs. B/T (Fig 5, Inset) are not superimposable on account of zero-field splitting (ZFS) effects. Thermogravimetric analysis, guest exchange and gas adsorption experiments The open framework structures of 2, 3 and 4 prompted an investigation into the possibility of evacuation and gas uptake in each of these materials. As the only network with three- dimensional connectivity, and the largest solvent-accessible volume, compound 2 was expected to display the most favourable properties. Thermogravimetric analysis of 2 showed the loss of the lattice solvent molecules (constituting 25% mass) by 100 ¬∞C, with a plateau region in the range 100 ‚Äì 300 ¬∞C, followed by a broad decomposition process. A sample of freshly isolated 2 was evacuated at 100 ¬∞C under dynamic vacuum and tested for gas adsorption properties. Due to the microporous nature of the structure, carbon dioxide was chosen as the probe gas, owing to its small size, favourable polarizability and high saturation pressure at 273K. Only minimal gas uptake was observed under these conditions, well below that expected from the structural analysis (maximum uptake ~7.5 ccSTP/g at 1 atm). X-ray powder diffraction of the sample following the adsorption experiment revealed that a transition to a new, poorly crystalline phase had taken place. In order to provide a more easily accessible desolvated material, solvent exchange experiments were attempted on 2 using acetonitrile and methanol as exchange solvents. However, gas adsorption experiments showed that the solvent-exchanged samples all failed to adsorb the significant amounts of CO2 at 273K (Figure S4, ESI) that would be expected from a microporous material of this nature, instead reaching maximum CO2 uptakes of a similar order to the original material. Measurement of an N2 adsorption isotherm on a representative sample of acetonitrile-exchanged 2 after evacuating at 100 ¬∞C under dynamic vacuum revealed similarly poor gas uptake in the micropore region and a correspondingly low calculated BET surface area of approximately 7 m2/g, with greater uptake in the pressure range 0.85-0.99 p/p0 consistent with some degree of mesopore character associated with the small crystallites. X-ray powder diffraction patterns of the solvent- exchanged samples, either prior to or following evacuation and gas adsorption experiments, were each consistent with the poorly crystalline phase isolated from direct thermal desolvation of 2. Despite extensive efforts, we were unable to obtain single crystals of this phase of sufficient quality for structural analysis. The thermogravimetric analysis plot obtained from a sample of the vacuum-desolvated 2 after standing in air for 96 hours showed a minimal mass loss in the range 30 ‚Äì 300 ¬∞C, consistent with only negligible re-adsorption of atmospheric water. Interestingly, the fresh material and the sample desolvated at 100 ¬∞C under high vacuum display a lower decomposition onset temperature of 300 ¬∞C, compared with the methanol and acetonitrile-exchanged materials, which undergo thermal decomposition with onset of approximately 350 ¬∞C. Comparison of the infrared spectra of the fresh and desolvated material shows loss of the infrared absorbances corresponding to the lattice water (3332 cm-1) and DMF molecules (1667 cm- 1), and shifts in the coordinating carboxylate absorbances in the range 1550-1650 cm-1, consistent with an altered coordination mode (Figure S5, ESI), while the remainder of the absorbances are retained. As such, it can be reasoned that the desolvated or solvent-exchanged forms of 2 consist of poorly ordered, close- packed structures arising from collapse of the solvent channels following desolvation. Thermogravimetric analysis of compound 3 revealed a broad mass loss profile, with 30 % mass lost in the range 100 ‚Äì 200 ¬∞C. On heating to 200 ¬∞C in air, the crystals of compound 3 slowly decomposed into a viscous oil, suggesting that a direct thermal activation of this material is not feasible. Given the low dimensionality, lack of strong inter-layer interactions, and large degree of solvent coordination present in the structure of 3, the inaccessibility of a stable, permanently porous phase would not be necessarily surprising. Nonetheless, solvent exchange experiments were conducted using acetonitrile. Although the exchange solvent was successful in displacing the lattice solvent molecules, indicated by thermogravimetric analysis, similarly to 2 the material displayed only very low CO2 uptake upon desolvation at either room temperature or 100 ¬∞C under dynamic vacuum (Figure S6, ESI). Discussion Comparing the structures of H4L and compounds 1 ‚Äì 4, a clear geometric preference for one of two broadly defined conformations is present. The compact, H-shaped conformation present in some degree within the structures of H4L, compound 1, compound 4, and one of the two crystallographically inequivalent units of compound 3 contains the potential for energetically favourable intramolecular œÄ-œÄ and C-H¬∑¬∑¬∑œÄ interactions. Conversely the extended X-shaped conformer, observed in compound 2 and one ligand within compound 3, achieved by rotation of two opposing N-CH2 bonds and the central H2C-N(Bz)2 bond, contains no potential for meaningful intramolecular interaction between the pendant phenyl groups. Given the high degree of conformational freedom within the structure of the ligand, a remarkable degree of consistency is observed, particularly in the case of the centrosymmetric conformers in the structures of 2, 3 and 4, which can be superimposed into two pairs of equivalent geometries (Figure 13). The ligand in compound 1 adopts a more distorted non- centrosymmetric orientation owing to the protonation of one tertiary amine site, resulting in the central N-CH2-CH2-N group adopting a non-planar geometry. Conclusions We have prepared four new coordination polymer materials from a novel tetracarboyxlic acid ligand H4L, and demonstrated for the first time the utility of the tetrabenzyl-alkylpolyamine skeleton in the synthesis of porous coordination polymers. The flexible backbone of the ligand L was shown to favour two distinct conformers when coordinated to metal ions in the solid state, which were translated as structural features into the resulting frameworks, and which may present a fertile new area in the continuing search for interesting, internally functionalised ligand molecules for applications in porous coordination polymer synthesis. The two-dimensional coordination polymer 1, containing trinuclear nickel cluster nodes with aqua and carboxylate bridging and a charge-balancing trialkylammonium cation within the ligand backbone, displayed ferromagnetic coupling behaviour and a positive zero-field splitting D parameter, which were fully described through experimental fitting and ab initio calculations. Compounds 2, 3 and 4, all ZnII coordination polymers containing prominent solvent channels within their structures, were probed through single-crystal and X-ray powder diffraction studies, solvent exchange and thermal analysis, and gas adsorption experiments. The combination of these techniques revealed that compounds 2 and 3 both underwent a structural transition to poorly crystalline materials of low porosity upon removal of the lattice solvent molecules, either thermally or by solvent exchange. In contrast, compound 4, despite displaying a much smaller solvent-accessible volume and solvent channels of very narrow limiting diameter, displayed drastically improved gas adsorption properties and selective uptake of CO2 over N2 from single-component isotherms measured at 273K. These properties establish compound 4 as an uncommon example of a stable, porous metallosupramolecular architecture containing non- coordinating, covalently attached amine groups which displays selective carbon dioxide uptake.
