See DOI: 10.1039/x0xx00000x Received 00th January 20xx, Accepted 00th January 20xx DOI: 10.1039/x0xx00000x www.rsc.org/ Structural and luminescent modulation in 8-hydroxyquinolinate- based coordination polymers by varying dicarboxylic acids Guozan Yuan*, Guoli Hu, Weilong Shan, Suo Jin, Qingyun Gu and Jing Chen Two Zn(II) and Cd(II) coordination polymers [ZnL2·2DMF] (1) and [CdL(OAc)] (2) were first synthesized by treating a novel 2- substituted 8-hydroxyquinolinate ligand HL involving a pyridyl group with zinc or cadmium salts. Two dicarboxylic acids ligands (H2BDC=1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid; H2BPDC=4,4'-Biphenyldicarboxylic acid) are employed as secondary auxiliary ligands to perform a systematic study on the structure diversities in the M(II)−quinolinate frameworks. By introducing two dicarboxylate anions in the reaction system, four new polymers [Zn2L2(BDC)] (3), [Zn3L2I2(BPDC)·2MeOH·8H2O] (4), [Cd2L2(BDC)] (5) and [Cd2L2(BPDC)·2MeOH·4H2O] (6) were obtained. Complex 1 possesses a two-dimensional (2D) square grid containing meso-helical chains. Complex 2 is a 2D network fabricated by binuclear {Zn2} secondary building units (SBUs). Complexes 3 and 5 show a kind of 2D structure constructed by cyclic hexamers Zn6L4, which are divided in half by coordinated BDC. In complex 4, the BPDC ligands bridge the 1D M(II)−L chains into 2D layer structure. Complex 6 presents an interesting 3D structure, in which the BPDC ligands link binuclear Cd(II) units into many meso-helical chains along the a and b axis. The diverse structures of complexes 1-6 indicate that the skeletons of dicarboxylate anions play a important role in the assembly of such different frameworks. Moreover, distinct fluorescence properties (emission wavelength and lifetime) of the complexes 1−6 were observed in the solid state. Introduction Coordination polymers (CPs) are a unique class of crystalline solids comprised of metal ions/clusters and organic linkers that have been rapidly developed as a new type of multifunctional luminescent materials.1,2 The luminescence of CPs can not only arise from the metal centers and organic moieties, but can also be tuned by metal–organic charge transfer, and guest molecules within porous CPs.3-5 In addition, the formation of the 3D frameworks in CPs may effectively enhance conformational rigidity of organic linkers, which can lead to increased fluorescence lifetimes and quantum efficiencies.6,7 These features enable the electronic nature of luminescent CPs to be tailored for a particular application in fluorescent sensors, nonlinear optics, photocatalysis, displays, electroluminescent devices, and biomedical imaging.8 Although numerous luminescent coordination polymers have been synthesized, rational control at the molecular level in the fabrication of CPs with tunable structures and luminescent properties still remains a big challenge for chemists. In the assembly process of CPs, the judicious selection of characteristic organic ligands is proved to be one of the most effective ways to manipulate the versatile structures.9,10 Considerable interest has been laid on the chemistry of metal- quinolinate since the first report on the use of aluminum tris- 8-hydroxyquinoline (AlQ3) as an electro-transporting and emitting material in organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs).11-13 It has been demonstrated that the luminescent properties of 8-hydroxyquinolinolate-based complexes are not only related to organic ligands and the metal ions, but also are heavily dependent on the degree of aggregation (core structure) and intermolecular packing of this compounds.14 Nevertheless, homoleptic MQn-type complexes provide relatively limited possibilities for tuning their photophysical properties. In view of this, Lewinski and our groups have constructed a series of multinuclear complexes and CPs involving 8- hydroxyquinolinate ligands.15,16 As a continuous work to seek the luminescent metal- quinolinate coordination polymers, we desire to design some novel CPs with complex yet well-ordered and periodic pore architectures by using combinations of structurally distinct ligands. By this means, we have succeeded to report an novel 8-hydroxyquinolinolate ligand HL involving a pyridyl group from the commercially available 8-hydroxyquinaldine to fabricate two Zn(II) and Cd(II) coordination polymers 1 and 2. In order to explore the influence of dicarboxylic acid skeletons on the structures of the metal-L frameworks, two dicarboxylic acids ligands (H2BDC=1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid; H2BPDC=4,4'-Biphenyldicarboxylic acid) are employed as auxiliary ligands. Structural features for coordination polymers of 1-6. From the above description, the L ligands uniformly behave as the only organic spacers to connect the metal centers in the resulting networks of 1 and 2, and thus, their structural discrepancy should be mainly attributed to the different ability to form bonds with L ligands and atom radius of Zn(II) and Cd(II). Distrinct secondary building units (mononuclear Zn(II) in 1 and binuclear Cd(II) in 2) lead to their variant 2D and 3D pore architectures. Previous reports have demonstrated that using multiple topologically distinct ligands to construct the CPs with programmed structures and properties is an efficient synthesis strategy.17 In this way, we have fabricated four new CPs 3-6 using two benzenedicarboxyl acids as secondary auxiliary ligands. Complexes 3 and 5 possess 2D grid structure built around similar binuclear M(II) units in comparison with complex 2. The luminescent behaviors of six Zn(II) and Cd(II) complexes and their corresponding free HL ligand were explored in the solid state at room temperature. When excited with 360 nm light, the free HL ligand exhibits a strong emission peak at 517 nm (Fig. S25), which originated from charge transfer of the internal HL ligand. Complexes 1-6 exhibit intense photoluminescences with the emission maximum at 597, 624, 598, 600, 592, and 624 nm, respectively, upon excitation at 360, 380 or 400 nm (Fig. 15). Compared with the emission spectra of the corresponding ligand HL, varying degrees of redshifts of 80 nm in 1, 107 nm in 2 and 6, 72 nm in 3, 83 nm in 4, and 75 nm in 5 have been observed, which are considered to mainly arise from the coordination of metal centers to the ligand L. The incorporation of Zn(II) or Cd(II) effectively increases the mobility of the electron transition in backbone due to back-coupling π-bond between the metal ion center and L ligand, and decreases the electron transition energy of intraligand charge transfer.18 Moreover, the coordination of the ligand with Zn(II) or Cd(II) ions forms additional five- membered rings, which increases the π–π* conjugation length and the conformational coplanarity, accordingly reduces the energy gap between the π and π* molecular orbital of the ligand.19 From emission spectra of complexes 2 and 6, it was considered that the carboxylate ligands have no significant contribution to the fluorescence emission of complexes in the presence of L ligand. The obvious redshifts compared to those of other four complexes may be attributed to their Cd(II) center and close-packed supramolecular structure. Additionally, the photophysical properties of complexes 1-6 are comparable with those found in quinolinate-based multinuclear complexes.16 Moreover, the collaborative functionalities of inherent porosity and luminescent property have enabled luminescent CPs to be a very promising new type of sensing materials.8a Table 1. The CCDC numbers of 1-6 are 1412803-1412808, respectively. Conclusions Two new M(II)-quinolinate coordination polymers 1 and 2 have been successfully synthesized by solvethermal methods. Using two dicarboxylic acids as secondary auxiliary ligands, four polymers 3-6 involving two topologically distinct ligands were obtained. These results demonstrate that the M(II)- quinolinate topological system can be modulated by choosing different secondary ligands. In addition, the fluorescence properties of compounds HL, and 1-6 were explored in the solid state at room temperature. The distinct photophysical properties of 1-6 (emission bands and fluorescence lifetimes) can be attributed to their different metal centers and supramolecular structures. Accordingly, these results may further enrich our knowledge of structural topologies for coordination networks, and they also provide a synthetic strategy to tune the structures and luminescent properties for the design of novel functional coordination polymers.
