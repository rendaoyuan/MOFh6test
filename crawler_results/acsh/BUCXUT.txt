Schlueter*,† Materials Science DiVision, Argonne National Laboratory, Argonne, Illinois 60439, and X-ray Science DiVision, AdVanced Photon Source, Argonne National Laboratory, Argonne, Illinois 60439 ReceiVed March 30, 2009; ReVised Manuscript ReceiVed May 14, 2009 ABSTRACT: Two nanoporous metal-organic framework materials incorporating the exotridentate bridging ligand 3-amino-1,2,4- triazolate (AmTAZ) have been synthesized through variation of secondary bridging anions: [Zn3(AmTAZ)3S](NO3)·(H2O) (1·(H2O)) and Zn7(AmTAZ)8(CO3)2(OH)2·2(EtOH) (2·2(EtOH); EtOH ) ethanol). 1·(H2O) crystallizes in the cubic space group I23 and is constructed from triangular Zn3S units that are bridged through AmTAZ ligands into a cationic three-dimensional (3D) network with nitrate and water molecules residing in the cavities. 2·2(EtOH) crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c and shows a complex 3D network constructed from seven crystallographically unique zinc centers bridged by AmTAZ, carbonate, and hydroxide anions. The porous nature of both materials has been explored by thermogravimetric analysis, nitrogen sorption, and in situ synchrotron- based powder X-ray diffraction. Introduction The synthesis and characterization of metal-organic frame- works (MOFs) with intricate structures and various function- alities continue to receive major attention, linking various disciplines of chemistry with materials science.1-5 The versatil- ity of these materials, from the perspectives of both design and function, has given rise to materials with a diverse range of physical and chemical properties (e.g., porosity,1,6 chirality,3,7 magnetic ordering,8 electronic switching,9 and anomalous thermal expansion behavior10), with potential applications in industrially and environmentally important areas, such as molecular sensing and gas storage/separation.11 Typically, MOFs have been constructed from transition metal ions and bridging ligands with carboxyl,6 pyridyl,12 or carbonitrile5 donor groups. However, the sustained exponential growth in MOF discovery has led to the utilization of an increasingly diverse range of bridging ligands with often speciﬁcally targeted chemical or geometric properties. In particular, ligands based on ﬁve- membered heterocycles, such as imidazole13,14 and tetrazole,15 have recently been shown to be highly effective at generating porous MOFs with network structures that mimic those of zeolites. For example, a series of zeolite-like MOFs (ZMOFs) constructed from bis(bidentate) imidazoledicarboxylic acid ligands with various metal ions have been reported that represent a molecular extension of traditional zeolite structural topologies, exhibiting large cavities and high chemical stabilities.13,16 Of key relevance here, however, are MOFs constructed with the bridging ligand 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (HAmTAZ).17-20 AmTAZ and other simple 1,2,4-triazole-based ligands have a well documented ability to generate polynuclear coordination compounds with diverse and often highly complex structures as a result of the various possible bi- and tridentate coordination modes (Scheme 1).21 While many binary materials are known to from with univalent metal ions, triazolate-based materials with divalent metal ions typically incorporate additional anionic ligands.17,18,22,23 Most commonly, these secondary anions are terminal halide ligands or other coordinating anions (e.g., nitrate or formate) that result from the metal salt used in the synthetic procedure. In other cases these secondary anions add further structural complexity by acting as a second bridging unit (e.g., hydroxide, oxalate, or terephthalate). Here, we report the synthesis and detailed characterization of two nanoporous MOFs incorporating AmTAZ with either sulﬁde, [Zn3(AmTAZ)3S]- (NO3)·(H2O) (1·(H2O)), or carbonate and hydroxide anions, Zn7(AmTAZ)8(CO3)2(OH)2·2(EtOH) (2·2(EtOH)). We previ- ously reported preliminary structural data for the former.24 The host-guest properties of both materials have been investigated using a range of techniques including in situ synchrotron-based powder X-ray diffraction. In recent years, the focus of MOF-related research efforts has begun to shift from targeted exploratory synthesis ap- proaches to more applied ﬁelds that exploit their porous natures.1,2,4 As such, the accurate elucidation of their often complex host-guest properties now presents a crucial step in their advancement. This necessitates the development of in situ structural techniques, such as X-ray diffraction, to precisely monitor the structural response during guest desorption and sorption processes.19,25,26 These techniques promise an unparal- leled insight into not only major structural changes, such as phase transformations, but also many other more subtle structural variants, such as those associated with framework dynamics and the rearrangement of host-guest interactions. All reagents were purchased from commercial sources and used without further puriﬁcation. The phase purity was conﬁrmed for each product using powder X-ray diffraction. Synthesis of [Zn3(AmTAZ)3S](NO3)·(H2O) (1·(H2O)). Zinc nitrate (297 mg, 1 mmol), potassium thiocyanate (97 mg, 1 mmol), and HAmTAZ (84 mg, 1 mmol) were combined in a 23 mL Teﬂon-lined Parr autoclave with 6 mL of H2O and 4 mL of ethanol (pH ) 4.6). The autoclave was sealed and heated at 140 °C for 7 days under autogenous pressure before cooling to room temperature at 0.1 °C min-1. Colorless crystals of 1·(H2O) were isolated by ﬁltration and washed once each with water (10 mL) and ethanol (10 mL). Synthesis of Zn7(AmTAZ)8(CO3)2(OH)2·2(EtOH) (2·2(EtOH)). Zinc carbonate hydroxide (275 mg, 0.5 mmol) and HAmTAZ (210 mg, 2.5 mmol) were combined in a 23 mL Teﬂon-lined Parr autoclave with 6 mL of H2O and 4 mL of ethanol (pH ) 6.5). The autoclave was sealed and heated at 160 °C for 3 weeks under autogenous pressure before cooling to room temperature at 0.1 °C min-1. Colorless crystals of 2·2(EtOH) were isolated by ﬁltration and washed once each with water (10 mL) and ethanol (10 mL). Thermogravimetry. Measurements were carried out on a TA Instruments Q50 Thermogravimetric Analyzer. To identify the ap- proximate temperature of guest loss and thermal decomposition the temperature was ramped at 2 °C min-1 from 20 to 500 °C under a dry dinitrogen atmosphere. For sorption measurements, samples of 2·2(EtOH) were desorbed ex situ by heating in an oven at 150 °C for 24 h and then immersed in water or ethanol for an additional 24 h. Figures for these data are included in the Supporting Information. Single Crystal X-ray Diffraction. Single crystals of 1·(H2O) and 2·2(EtOH) were glued to the end of a glass ﬁber and mounted on a Siemens SMART X-ray diffractometer equipped with a CCD area detector and a sealed-tube Mo KR (λ ) 0.71073 Å) X-ray source with a graphite monochromator. The area detector frames were integrated by use of the program SAINT,27 and the resulting intensities corrected for absorption by Gaussian integration based on the measured crystal shape using the program XPREP within the SAINT suite. Other systematic variations were corrected by the analysis of replicate reﬂections using the program SADABS.28 The structures were solved by use of direct methods, while full-matrix least-squares reﬁnement on F2 (including all data) were performed, both using the program package SHELXTL.29 All ordered non-hydrogen atoms were reﬁned anisotropically. Positions for most hydrogen atoms were calculated by employing a “riding” model. Positions for the hydrogen atoms of water molecules, hydroxide anions, and the hydroxyl groups of ethanol molecules were located from the electron density difference map. No correction for extinction was required. Solvent accessible volumes were calculated within PLATON30 using the VOID routine. Synchrotron-Based Powder X-ray Diffraction (S-PXRD). Poly- crystalline samples of 1·(H2O) and 2·2(EtOH) were loaded into polyimide capillaries (0.9 mm diameter) and mounted in a ﬂow cell apparatus32 under a ﬂow of helium gas. The samples were continuously maintained under either neat helium or vapor-saturated (water or ethanol) helium gas ﬂow for the duration of the experiment. The X-rays (20.15 keV, 0.61516 Å) available at the 1-BM beamline at the Advanced Photon Source at Argonne National Laboratory were used in combina- tion with a MAR-345 imaging plate detector to record diffraction patterns. The sample temperature was controlled using an Oxford Cryosystems 700 plus cryostat and data were collected in 5 s exposures upon continuous ramping over the range 0-225 °C, at 100 °C h-1. This corresponds to the collection of diffraction images at 3 K intervals. The raw images were processed using Fit-2D.33 The sample-to-detector distance and tilt of the imaging plate relative to the beam were reﬁned using a LaB6 standard. Le Bail analyses of the variable-temperature diffraction data were performed within GSAS.34 Details of the reﬁnements are included in the Supporting Information. Nitrogen Sorption Measurements. Nitrogen sorption experiments were measured at -196 °C with a Quantachrome Autosorb-1C volumetric gas sorption instrument. Samples were outgassed at 110 °C under a high vacuum (∼10-7 Torr) for at least 24 h prior to the measurements. Figures and additional details for these data are included in the Supporting Information. Results and Discussion Synthesis. Hydro- or solvothermal syntheses represent one of the most versatile and widely used approaches for the preparation of MOFs.1,2 One relatively unexplored aspect of this synthetic approach is the in situ generation of novel ligands via decomposition pathways, as exempliﬁed here for the preparation of 1·(H2O) where sulﬁde is produced from the decomposition of thiocyanate. This is in contrast with our earlier report of the nonporous network Zn4S(1,2,4-triazolate)3(NCS)3 where, under similar reaction conditions to that for 1·(H2O), the thiocyanate anions were readily incorporated into the framework, while both the sulﬁde and triazolate anions resulted from the decomposition of 3-mercapto-1,2,4-triazole.35 Related approaches whereby sulﬁde anions are generated through the decomposition of thiourea have been reported for the incorpora- tion of sulﬁde cluster components in solid-state inorganic materials.36 There have been very few sulﬁde-containing MOFs reported to date,35,37 possibly due to the insolubility of direct sulﬁde precursors. However, approaches such as those described here offer a convenient route to such systems with the presumably slow decomposition pathways likely to provide a controlled release of reagents and promote the growth of single crystals. The synthesis of phase pure products of both 1·(H2O) and 2·2(EtOH) required extended reactions times compared to typical hydrothermal syntheses for MOFs (e.g., 1-3 days). The decomposition pathway for 1·(H2O) required reaction times of around one week to give highly crystalline and phase pure material. Reaction times of at least 3 weeks were necessary for 2·2(EtOH), with PXRD analysis of products from shorter reactions indicating that signiﬁcant amounts of the moderately insoluble zinc carbonate hydroxide starting material remained. This approach for the incorporation of carbonate anions into zinc-triazolate-based frameworks contrasts that used by Chen and co-workers, where the combination of AmTAZ with zinc nitrate and sodium bicarbonate resulted in a dramatically different pillared-layer structure.17 Single Crystal X-ray Diffraction. Crystals of 1·(H2O) crystallize in the cubic space group I23. The basic structural building unit is a distorted SZn3 trigonal pyramid where the sulfur atom resides at the apical vertex and bonds to three crystallographically equivalent Zn atoms along the edges (Figure 1a). The tetrahedral coordination environment of the zinc centers are completed by triazolate nitrogen atoms of the bridging AmTAZ ligands. The AmTAZ ligands bridge zinc centers both within (N1 and N2) and between (N3) the SZn3 units, resulting in a highly connected three-dimensional (3D) network (Figure 1b). Disordered nitrate anions and guest water molecules occupy the network cavities that account for 40.7% of the total crystal volume. Compound 2·2(EtOH) crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. The complex 3D structure is described by an asymmetric unit containing seven unique zinc coordination environments and eight AmTAZ ligands, as well as two carbonate and two hydroxide anions. A Zn3(AmTAZ)6 cluster consisting of the sole octahedral zinc center (Zn3) sandwiched between two tetrahedral zinc centers (Zn2 and Zn4) provides the core building unit of the structure (Figure 2a). The central octahedral zinc coordinates to each of the six AmTAZ ligands in the Zn3(AmTAZ)6 cluster. Each of these AmTAZ ligands then bridges to one of the two adjacent zinc centers with an oxygen atom from either a bridging carbonate or hydroxide anion completing the tetrahedral coordination sphere. The Zn3(AmTAZ)6 cluster bridges in eight directions, through the six AmTAZ ligands and the carbonate and hydroxide anions, to two each of the four remaining tetrahedral zinc centers (ZnN3O: Zn1 and Zn6; ZnN2O2: Zn5 and Zn7) (Figure 2b). These zinc centers bridge the Zn3(AmTAZ)6 clusters directly forming dense chains along the c-axis. The chains of clusters are then linked by additional carbonate and hydroxide anions along the a-axis, and a further two AmTAZ ligands along the b-axis (Figure 3a). While all the AmTAZ ligands associated with the Zn3(AmTAZ)6 clusters are µ3-bridging, these additional two AmTAZ ligands that connect the clusters exhibit µ2-bridging modes. The µ2-bridging mode is more commonly associated with the protonated form of AmTAZ,23 which in this case would suggest that in terms of charge balance the hydroxide anions may alternatively be oxide anions. However, the consistency between equivalent bond lengths within the µ2- compared to the µ3-bridging AmTAZ ligands combined with bond order calculations38 for the hydroxide oxygen atoms conﬁrm the present structural model. The resulting highly connected 3D framework deﬁnes a constricted triply interpenetrated pore structure that accounts for 28.3% of the total crystal volume (Figure 3b). This pore network consists primarily of two sets of one-dimensional (1D) zig-zagging pores that run along the c-axis (2.9 × 3.6 Å2) and the ac-plane (2.6 × 4.7 Å2). These 1D pores are then connected by short linear pore sections along the b-axis (∼2.7 × 3.8 × 17 Å3) to give a partial 3D character to the overall pore network. The relative changes in the reﬁned cubic lattice volume for 1·(H2O) accompanying the desorption and resorption of water are shown in Figure 4. No symmetry changes were apparent during these processes. Upon heating, there is initially a small, abrupt decrease of ca. 0.14% in the lattice volume up to 40 °C that is consistent with the loss of easily liberated water molecules. Beyond this, there is a brief period of thermal expansion up to 70 °C, followed by two distinct regions of lattice contraction as the remaining water molecules are slowly released - very gradual water loss from 70 to 190 °C, and more rapid release between 190 and 223 °C - to give a total volume contraction of ca. 0.27%. Upon cooling in the presence of water vapor, the now desorbed host material (1) exhibits approximately linear thermal contraction behavior down to ca. 90 °C. This is followed by a smaller region of near zero thermal contraction between 90 and 50 °C where, presumably, a slight amount of water starts to be resorbed that counters the lattice volume changes associated with thermal contraction. Below 50 °C there is a dramatic increase in lattice volume of ca. 0.5% as the water vapor is resorbed into the material. The lattice volume stabilizes below 10 °C with values comparable to that of the virgin sample, suggesting an essentially complete resorption process. The relative changes in the reﬁned monoclinic lattice volume for 2·2(EtOH) accompanying the desorption of ethanol, and the subsequent resorption of both ethanol and water, are shown in Figure 5 (percentage changes are reported with respect to the fully desorbed material, 2, at 202 °C). Again, no symmetry changes were apparent during these processes. The change in lattice volume associated with the initial desorption of ethanol shows, ﬁrst, an abrupt decrease of ca. 0.5% between 55 and 70 °C, followed by a more gradual decrease of an additional ca. 0.2% up to 100 °C. Upon further heating to 202 °C the now desorbed material, 2, exhibits a small degree of thermal expansion. The changes in lattice volume upon cooling in the presence of either ethanol or water vapor show similar behavior, where sorption is conﬁrmed in both cases by a smooth increase between 125 and 50 °C. Interestingly, the ﬁnal lattice volume increase of 0.76% for ethanol exceeds that of the virgin ethanol loaded material. This may be the result of one or a combination of several factors: ﬁrst, the apparently lower volume for the virgin sample may indicate that a signiﬁcant amount of desorption occurred during sample loading; additionally, this may indicate a differing alignment of guest molecules in the pore network and that the resorption process is not precisely reversible. The uptake of water results in a larger overall volume increase than that observed for ethanol uptake, with the relative change in lattice volume reaching a maximum of 0.98% at 20 °C. This is perhaps expected given the highly constricted nature of the pore network in 2, which may be more easily negotiated by the smaller water molecules. A more detailed understanding of the structural response to the sorption of ethanol and water can be gained by also considering the relative changes in each of the monoclinic lattice parameters (Figure 6). Thermogravimetry. For 1·(H2O), thermogravimetry reveals an initial mass decrease associated with the loss of guest water molecules up to 50 °C, beyond which the mass remains stable until the onset of thermal decomposition at approximately 280 °C. The initial mass loss of approximately 5% is greater than the calculated value of 3.2% for one water molecule per formula unit as observed for the X-ray structural determination. This suggests a degree of desorption during the diffraction data collection process at ambient temperature and is consistent with the observation of low partial occupations for the water sites. Thermogravimetric analysis of 2·2(EtOH) shows a broad initial mass loss up to approximately 130 °C before thermally decomposing beyond 220 °C. The initial mass loss of ap- proximately 8% is consistent with desorption of two ethanol molecules per formula unit (calculated: 6.7%). The ex-situ resorption of ethanol and water into the desorbed host material 2 was conﬁrmed by thermogravimetry, whereby the ethanol and water resorbed samples showed respective mass losses of approximately 5 and 3% associated with the subsequent removal of the guest molecules. Nitrogen Sorption. No sorption of nitrogen was observed for 1. A small uptake of nitrogen was observed for 2, reaching a maximum uptake of ∼42 cm3 g-1 near saturation pressure. The sorption characteristics for 2 are broadly consistent with type-I behavior with a clear micropore region at low partial pressures. However, no plateau is reached at higher partial pressures suggesting slow uptake kinetics as would be expected for the constricted 1D pore structure of 2 where the pore window dimensions closely match those of the nitrogen adsorbate molecules. The Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) surface area for 2 was estimated to be 30 m2/g, but it is likely that the slow kinetics of sorption make this result uncertain. In addition to investigating the sorption of smaller gases (e.g., H2) in the future, sorption studies at higher temperatures (e.g., CO2 at ambient temperature) may also be pertinent as it seems likely that the high degree of structural ﬂexibility observed for 2 may result in a signiﬁcantly relaxed (or “closed”) pore structure at low temperatures. Conclusion 1·(H2O) represents a highly unusual example of a MOF containing sulﬁde bridging anions, achieved here by the in situ formation of sulﬁde ions during the solvothermal synthesis. More generally, such a synthetic approach may provide a convenient route to generating novel sulﬁde-containing frame- works in the future. Although no nitrogen uptake was observed for 1, nanoporosity was conﬁrmed through in situ synchrotron- based X-ray diffraction studies of desorption and resorption of the guest water molecules. The cubic symmetry of the system was maintained throughout these processes revealing a subtle, concerted “breathing” of the host framework. The inclusion of three unique types of anionic bridging ligands in 2·2(EtOH) has generated an intricate 3D network structure that deﬁnes a highly convoluted, and primarily 1D, pore system. Nitrogen sorption studies of the desorbed host material, 2, indicate that the passage of nitrogen through the pore system is signiﬁcantly hindered, suggesting that this material may be well suited for gas separation applications, especially as related to hydrogen puriﬁcation. In dramatic contrast to 1, in situ synchrotron-based X-ray diffraction studies of 2 revealed highly anisotropic structural ﬂexing during the desorption and sorption of both ethanol and water molecules, due in part to the increased degrees of freedom imparted by its complex, lower symmetry structure. Interestingly, the uptake of the different guest molecules proceeds via subtly different structural ﬂexing mechanisms, which leads to a larger overall lattice volume increase for the sorption of the smaller water molecules. This work further demonstrates the importance of in situ structural studies for the precise elucidation of structure-property relationships in MOFs and other advanced porous materials. Single-crystal X-ray Diffraction - Details of crystallographic modeling. 4.1 - [Zn3(AmTAZ)3S](NO3)·(H2O) (1·(H2O)): The asymmetric unit includes a ⅓ occupied sulfide, a fully occupied zinc center and AmTAZ ligand, aswell as a disordered nitrate/water site and two partially occupied water sites in the cavity. No disorder was evident in the cationic framework component of 1·(H2O), and all non-hydrogen framework were atoms were modeled anisotropically. The nitrate and water components of the shared disordered site (centered at ½, x, 0) were fixed to respective occupancies of ⅓ and 1/6 in order to balance the charge of the system and give the required (symmetry defined) total occupation of ½ at the site. This describes a 2:1 disordered arrangement of nitrate anions and water molecules throughout the cavities of the structure. The nitrogen atom of the nitrate anion (N5) and the oxygen atom (O10) of the water molecule were fixed to have the same atomic coordinates and displacement parameter (EXYZ/EADP). Two additional guest water molecule sites (O20 and O30) were located from the difference map and fixed to have respective estimated partial occupations of 0.06667 and 0.1 with refined atomic displacement parameters of ~0.15. These estimated partial occupations give the best approximation to electron density population calculations from the SQUEEZE routine within the PLATON suite.1 Treatment of a structural model that excluded the two partially occupied water sites with the SOLVENT routine within PLATON revealed a residual solvent accessible volume of ~28%. All atoms of the disordered nitrate/water site and the two additional guest water molecules were modeled isotropically. S14 4.2 - [Zn7(AmTAZ)8(CO3)2(OH)2·2(EtOH) (2·2(EtOH)): The asymmetric unit contains seven zinc centers, eight AmTAZ ligands, two carbonate anions, two hydroxide anions, and two ethanol guest molecules. All ordered framework atoms were modeled anisotropically. Two-fold positional disorder was evident for the amino group of one AmTAZ ligand (N84) and also the non-coordinating oxygen atom of one carbonate anion (O8). In both cases, the A and B components were fixed to have the same isotropic atomic displacements parameters and their relative occupancies refined to be ca. 0.231(18):0.769(18) and 0.365(12):0.635(12) respectively for N84 and O8. Electron density population calculations from the SQUEEZE routine within the PLATON suite suggested a guest molecule occupation of close to two ethanol molecules per formula unit. While two ethanol guest molecule sites were apparent from the difference map, a satisfactory model could not be achieved using soft constraints alone. Consequently, the two sites were modeled using the FRAG command from the carbon and oxygen atom coordinates of a well ordered ethanol molecule from a previously reported structure.2 All atoms of the ethanol guest molecules were modeled isotropically.
